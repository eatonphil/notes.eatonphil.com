# Academic- or industry-born? A programming language survey ## February 8, 2022 ###### interpreters,compilers,history,survey Developers often think of compiler and interpreter development as being pretty academic, or at least requiring advanced degrees to be successful. Then again there's a common concept of a language spectrum from academic (e.g. Scheme, Standard ML, Haskell) to business (e.g. Python, C++). So let's take a look at the [TIOBE](https://www.tiobe.com/tiobe-index/) top programming languages and dig into the background of their creation. One major caveat in this post is that to keep scope narrower I'll not go into the post-creation development of the language and implementation. It's possible that the post-creation development skews in its own direction academic- or industry-led. Also, I'm going to be looking into this from public sources so if there are mistakes please let me know! ### Python: Academic; Master's degree This is surprising! I just listed Python as "obviously" being more on the business side of things but it was actually created by van Rossum (Master's degree) while at a research institute. Sources * [The Making of Python](https://www.artima.com/articles/the-making-of-python) ### C: Industry; PhD C was created by Dennis Ritchie (PhD) at Bell Labs. But the focus on the language was to help build utilities for UNIX and eventually to rewrite UNIX. UNIX itself was a commercial operating system, even if it's initial customers were research groups. Sources: * [The Development of the C Language](http://www.bell-labs.com/usr/dmr/www/chist.html) * [UNIX History](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unix#History) ### Java: Industry; PhD Java was created by James Gosling (PhD) at Sun Microsystems. Sources: * [Java Technology: The Early Years](https://web.archive.org/web/20050420081440/http://java.sun.com/features/1998/05/birthday.html) ### C++: Industry; PhD Started by Bjarne Stroustrup (PhD) at (or just before joining?)
